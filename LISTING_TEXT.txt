### The Problem

You know that soul-crushing feeling when you need to scatter 50 objects naturally? Moving each one by hand, rotating it, adjusting height, trying to make it look "random but not too random"? Yeah, I was done with that.

### The Solution

Physics Dropper is basically "let gravity handle it" turned into a workflow. Select your objects, click Drop, watch them fall into realistic positions. Not happy? Adjust the physics settings, add some force fields, run it again. When it looks good, bake it to keyframes and you're done.

It's like Drop-It on steroids (but way more stable and with a lot more control).

### How It Works

**Basic Workflow:**
1. Select the mesh objects you want to place
2. Hit "Drop" (or press V)
3. Play the simulation and watch physics do its magic
4. Not quite right? Pause, tweak settings, try again
5. Perfect? Hit "Apply" (Shift+V) to bake everything

**The Fun Stuff:**

**Rigidbody Physics** â€“ Objects fall, bounce, collide, and settle naturally. Adjust mass, friction, bounciness, collision shapesâ€”whatever you need. Got high-poly meshes? Enable proxy mode and it'll create optimized collision shapes automatically.

**Cloth Simulation** â€“ Turn objects into fabric with one click. Choose from material presets (I've tested these extensively: Cotton for general fabric, Denim for heavier stuff, Silk for flowing materials, etc.). Or go wild with the advanced settings if you want precise control.

**Interactive Force Fields** â€“ This is my favorite part. Press F to enter force mode, move your mouse around to position a force field in 3D space, scroll to adjust its radius. Hold Ctrl and click to snap it to surfaces. It's super intuitive once you try it.

**Earthquake Effects** â€“ Want everything to shake randomly? Enable earthquake mode and adjust intensity. Perfect for adding life to static scenes or creating destruction setups.
